# Viktor Shklovsky — Wikipedia EN (cross-referenced with primary texts)

Viktor Borisovich Shklovsky (24 January 1893, Saint Petersburg — 6 December 1984, Moscow). Founder of Russian Formalism; the concept of *ostranenie* (defamiliarization) in literary theory.

## TL;DR / Abstract

> **Russian Formalist, 1893–1984.** Founder of the concept of **ostranenie** (defamiliarization) in literature. Wrote «Искусство как приём» ("Art as Technique" / "Art as Device", 1917) and «О теории прозы» ("Theory of Prose", 1925). Analyzed Tolstoy's literary technique in «Лев Толстой» (1963).

This is the **theoretical foundation** of Lever 12. The Wikipedia article gives the biographical and bibliographical scaffolding; the *key claims* of ostranenie are derived from the primary texts quoted below, not the encyclopedia. Where the Wikipedia article is silent, the Russian originals are cited directly.

## Key concepts (Shklovsky)

### 1. Ostranenie (defamiliarization) — the central term

The full original quotation from «Искусство как приём» (1917):

> «Целью искусства является дать как чувство вещи, как видение, а не как узнавание; приёмом искусства является приём «остранения» вещей и приём затруднённой формы, увеличивающие трудность и длительность восприятия, ибо восприятие в искусстве самоцельно и должно быть растянуто до предела.»

English (Richard Sherwood, 1965 translation):

> «The purpose of art, then, is to lead us to a knowledge of a thing through the organ of sight instead of recognition. By 'enstranging' objects and complicating form, the device of art makes perception long and 'laborious.' The perceptual process in art has a purpose all its own and ought to be extended to the fullest. Art is a means of experiencing the process of creativity.»

Three claims packed into this passage:

1. **Perception, not recognition.** Art makes us *see*, not *identify*. Recognition is automatic; perception is effortful.
2. **Form is the means.** Ostranenie is achieved through *form* (syntax, layout, structure), not content.
3. **Effort is the point.** A text that is *easy* to read is not yet art. The labor of reading is the experience.

### 2. The fabula/syuzhet distinction

From «О теории прозы» (1925):

> «Сюжет Стерна богат отступлениями ровно настолько, насколько он беден событиями.» (Sterne's plot is rich in digressions exactly to the extent that it is poor in events.)

Shklovsky distinguishes:
- **Fabula** — the chronological sequence of events.
- **Syuzhet** — the way events are *told* (which can rearrange, defer, omit, juxtapose).

The point: literary meaning lives in *how* something is told, not *what* happened. Two identical events told in two different orders are two different stories.

### 3. Sterne as the master of defamiliarization

Shklovsky translated and wrote extensively about Laurence Sterne. Sterne's *Tristram Shandy* is the prime example: a novel that discusses its own composition, breaks the fourth wall, and uses digression as the primary structural device. For Shklovsky, Sterne is the precursor who *invented* the technique Shklovsky later named.

### 4. Tolstoy as the master of "defamiliarization through simple description"

From «Лев Толстой» (1963):

> «Я сейчас пользуюсь тем, что остранение и формализм не соединены в одном понятии. ... Можно остранение находить у Толстого, и именно при описании вещей, потому что у Толстого особенный материал: он не доверяет известным вещам.»

Shklovsky's analysis: Tolstoy uses *ordinary description* — saying what a familiar thing looks like as if seeing it for the first time — to achieve the defamiliarization effect. This is *not* ornamentation; it is *resetting perception*.

The example Shklovsky uses: Tolstoy describing the opera as "a row of bare-bottomed men jumping up and down on a stage, waving their arms and singing at the top of their voices." A familiar high-culture event, described as if by a naive witness, becomes strange.

### 5. The three levels of perception (Shklovsky's metatheory)

| Level | What happens | How the model fails |
|---|---|---|
| **Recognition** (узнавание) | Automatic, unconscious | LLM-typical: template reproduction. |
| **Vision** (видение) | Effortful, fresh perception | What we want: парцелляция, эллипсис, литота. |
| **Concept** (замысел) | The "idea" of the work | Shklovsky's claim: form is primary; concept follows form, not the reverse. |

For Shklovsky, form *creates* concept. A text's meaning is what its form forces the reader to see. This is the *anti-Platonic* claim: ideas are not pre-existing; they emerge from the form of the text.

## Specific techniques in Tolstoy that Shklovsky analyzes

Shklovsky catalogs five defamiliarization techniques in Tolstoy, paraphrased:

1. **Refusal to name the familiar object** (Tolstoy describes a chair by its geometry, not its name).
2. **Defamiliarization through a child's perspective** (Tolstoy describes the opera through Natasha Rostova's naive perception in *War and Peace*).
3. **Repetition of an event in different perspectives** (the duel in *War and Peace* told from Andrei's, then Pierre's, then Nikolai's view).
4. **Violation of expected syntactic flow** (parcels, fragments, lists where a continuous narration is expected).
5. **Use of anachronism or historical distance** (Tolstoy describing Napoleon's Russia with the flatness of a chronicle, making the familiar strange).

**This is the most direct theoretical basis for Lever 12 in our skills.** The five techniques map onto our four Russian brevity techniques:

| Tolstoy's defamiliarization move | Our Lever 12 technique |
|---|---|
| Refusal to name the familiar | Литота (преуменьшение через знакомое) |
| Child's perspective | Нулевая связка (минимализм) |
| Repetition in different perspectives | Эллипсис (читатель заполняет) |
| Violation of expected syntax | Парцелляция (расщепление) |
| Anachronism / distance | Эллипсис (гэппинг) |

## Other works by Shklovsky (relevant to literary theory)

- **«Zoo, or Letters Not About Love» (1923)** — epistolary novel where the form (letters) is *the* content. Each letter is a parcel; the structure makes a single coherent story emerge from discontinuous units.
- **«Хождение» / «A Sentimental Journey» (1923)** — memoirs, fragmented. Shklovsky uses the form to defamiliarize his own life.
- **«О литературе и кино» (On Literature and Cinema, 1923-1930s)** — applies ostranenie to film. The "montage of attractions" school (Eisenstein) is in part a Shklovian inheritance.
- **«За и против» (For and Against, 1957)** — Shklovsky's later essays, after the Formalist school was suppressed by Soviet power. He is in part reconsidering the Formalist project.

## Limitations (what the corpus does not give us)

- **Wikipedia EN is a tertiary source.** The original texts (Russian-language, 1917–1963) are the primary source. We quote them where the English summary is loose.
- **Theostrия ostranения** is a 20th-century Russian-specific term. It does not map directly onto Western literary terms (defamiliarization is a translation choice; "making strange" is a near-equivalent). Western literary theory (Burke's "fourth figure," the New Critics' "defamiliarization," Shklovsky's precursor) is a separate lineage.
- **Theostrия ostranения** is a *poetic* theory. Its application to *prose* is Shklovsky's extension in «О теории прозы». Our Lever 12 is prose-specific; the extension to prose is part of the theory, not its origin.
- **Shklovsky's late work** (post-1930) is in part a *reconsideration* of Formalism. He is not a pure Formalist by the 1960s. Theostrия ostranения as we use it is the *early* Shklovsky.

## Connection to our work

This is the **theoretical foundation** of Lever 12. Our four Russian brevity techniques — парцелляция, эллипсис, литота, нулевая связка — are *all* techniques of defamiliarization applied to prose.

| Lever 12 technique | How it is defamiliarization |
|---|---|
| **Парцелляция** (splitting) | The reader is forced to re-read each parcel; the act of reading is *laborious*. |
| **Эллипсис** (gapping) | The omitted element is *present* by its absence; the reader constructs it. |
| **Литота** (understatement) | The familiar is rendered unfamiliar by the disproportion between thing and words. |
| **Нулевая связка** (zero copula) | The grammatical minimum forces the reader to supply the relation. |

**What Shklovsky's theory says about our skills (in one paragraph):**

Each Russian brevity technique in Lever 12 is an instance of the *ostranение* operation: form the reader to perceive a familiar thing as if anew. The theory is 1917; the techniques are folkloric (oral tradition in Russian uses парцелляция and литота extensively); our skill *names* and *counts* them. The theory is not the limit of what we can do — it's the floor.

**What Shklovsky's theory does NOT give us:**

- It does not give us a **metric** for whether a text is "defamiliarized enough." Shklovsky's framework is qualitative. Our `humanize-editor` must add quantitative checks (e.g., sentence-length variance, low-entropy word choice).
- It does not give us a **register check.** Defamiliarization works in literary prose; it fails in legal, medical, and onboarding documents. The Averintsev critique applies (see our `averintsev-rhetoric-genre.md`).
- It does not give us a **transferable mechanism** for non-Russian languages. English and Chinese have their own defamiliarization traditions. Our skill is RU-only; cross-language transfer is a separate problem.

## Open questions

- **Empirical measurement of ostranenie.** Can we measure "perceptual effort" in a corpus? Sentence-length variance is a proxy, but a weak one. A better metric might be: time to first semantic parse, or surprisal rate. Not measured in our corpus.
- **Cross-language transfer.** Does the same theory apply to English? English *poetry* uses similar devices (Whitman's catalog, Dickinson's dashes), but English *prose* conventions (formal, business, academic) are more conservative. Lever 12 in English would need its own catalogue.
- **LLM as defamiliarization detector.** Could an LLM judge whether a paragraph is "defamiliarized" or "automatic"? Current LLMs are themselves partly *trained* on the automaticity Shklovsky critiques; they may be biased judges. This is the same Park 2024 / Zhang 2024 problem in a different register.
- **Relation to negative capability.** Keats's notion of "negative capability" (the capacity to remain in uncertainty without reaching for fact and reason) is the closest English Romantic parallel. Whether the two notions are formally equivalent is an open literary-history question.

## Raw notes

- **Биография.** Шкловский родился в 1893 в Санкт-Петербурге. Учился на юридическом и историко-филологическом факультетах Петроградского университета. Основатель и теоретик ОПОЯЗ (Общество изучения поэтического языка) в 1916–1917. После революции 1917 переключился на литературную критику, документальную литературу, киносценарии. Братья Шкловского расстреляны (1918, 1937). Жена — Нина Бернштейн, литературовед.
- **Школа.** ОПОЯЗ (1916–1930) — основная школа. Параллельно Московский лингвистический кружок (Роман Якобсон и др.) — структурализм в смежной формулировке. Шкловский и Якобсон разошлись к 1930.
- **«Искусство как приём» (1917).** Опубликовано в сборнике «Поэтика: Сборники по теории поэтического языка» (1919). Главный текст русского формализма.
- **«О теории прозы» (1925).** Сборник эссе о Стерне, Толстом, рассказе и сюжете. Книга, в которой Шкловский развивает «остранение» применительно к прозе.
- **«Лев Толстой» (1963).** Поздняя книга о Толстом, в которой Шкловский применяет формалистский метод к русской классике.
- **MLA Prize (2017).** Modern Language Association дала Шкловскому (посмертно) премию за вклад в литературоведение — высокая западная оценка позднего формализма.
- **Перевод на английский.** Benjamin Sher перевёл «Искусство как приём» и часть «О теории прозы» в 1990.
